Indian Classical Music is a rich, modal musical system that spans thousands of years. While Western music is heavily focused on chord progressions and harmony, Indian classical music focuses on **melody** and **microtones**.

Translating this sound to the guitar can open up new sonic palettes for your solos, fusion projects, and acoustic playing. In this guide, we will break down the absolute basics of Indian Ragas on the guitar.

---

## 1. The Concept of Swaras (Notes)

In Indian music, notes are called **Swaras**. Instead of C-D-E-F-G-A-B, the Indian solfege system (Sargam) uses:
* **Sa** (Tonic / Root note)
* **Re** (Second)
* **Ga** (Third)
* **Ma** (Fourth)
* **Pa** (Fifth)
* **Dha** (Sixth)
* **Ni** (Seventh)

Unlike Western music, where the root note changes when you modulate keys, the note **Sa** is always your fixed root. You select a drone key (e.g., E), and all other notes are played relative to E.

---

## 2. The 10 Thaats (Parent Scales)

In Hindustani classical music, ragas are categorized under 10 parent scales called **Thaats**. Think of these as the Indian equivalent to the 7 diatonic modes.

Here are two common Thaats you can play on your guitar today:

### Thaat Bilawal (The Major Scale)
This is identical to the Western Major scale (Ionian Mode). All notes are natural.
* **Sargam:** Sa Re Ga Ma Pa Dha Ni
* **Formula:** 1 2 3 4 5 6 7

### Thaat Yaman / Kalyan (The Lydian Mode)
This scale uses a sharp 4th note (Teevra Ma). It creates an ethereal, meditative sound.
* **Sargam:** Sa Re Ga Ma# Pa Dha Ni
* **Formula:** 1 2 3 #4 5 6 7

## 3. Adapting Ragas on the Fretboard

Playing a Raga is more than just running up and down a scale. A Raga has specific rules:
* **Arohana:** The ascending path (the notes you are allowed to play going up).
* **Avarohana:** The descending path (the notes you play going down).
* **Vadi:** The most important, emphasized note in the Raga.
* **Samvadi:** The second most important note (usually a perfect fifth or fourth away from the Vadi).

For example, in **Raga Bhupali** (a beautiful pentatonic scale), you are not allowed to play the 4th (Ma) or 7th (Ni) notes. The Arohana and Avarohana are identical: `Sa - Re - Ga - Pa - Dha - Sa'`.

## Tips for Guitarists

1. **Use a Drone:** Since there are no chord changes, play with a constant background drone (a Tanpura app or a simple open E-string drone). This makes the note intervals stand out.
2. **Practice Legato (Meend):** Indian classical music is famous for slides and microtones. Use glissando slides and smooth hammer-ons/pull-offs to mimic the vocal-like slides (Meend) of the Sitar or Veena.
